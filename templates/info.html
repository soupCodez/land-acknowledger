Info

  • Health Disparities

    Indigenous children have higher rates of respiratory issues and infectious diseases. This is partly due to substandard housing and inadequate, overcrowded living conditions.

  • Educational Attainment

    Canada has high educational attainment but Indigenous students graduate less. Only 48% students of reserves graduate from high school compared to 75% for those off-reserve.

  • Housing Conditions

    About 16.4% of Indigenous peoples live in homes requiring major repairs. This is almost three times higher than for people with non-Indigenous background. 17.1% of Indigenous households were crowded.

  • Income Levels

    Indigenous Canadians can earn only about 70 cents for each dollar earned by a non-Indigenous Canadian. In cities, Indigenous workers can earn 34% less than non-Indigenous workers doing similar jobs. On reserves, non-Indigenous workers can make up to 88% more than Indigenous workers.

  • Employment/Unemployment Rates

    Higher unemployment among Indigenous peoples is partially attributed to the generally lower levels of educational attainment. Some of the barriers include cultural differences and discrimination, but also poverty, lack of access to transportation, and inability to find childcare.

  • Incarceration Rates

    Indigenous people make up 32% of all prisoners but comprise about 5% of all Canadians. Indigenous women represent nearly half of all women incarcerated in federal prisons.

  • Premature Deaths & Unintentional Injuries

    Indigenous children and youth experience a higher rate of unintentional injuries when compared to non-Indigenous youth. Most isolated reserves lack health human resources to rehabilitate the injured.

  • Suicide Rates

    Canadian Indigenous communities have some of the highest recorded suicide rates worldwide. Suicide is one of the highest causes of death for First Nations youth and adults.
